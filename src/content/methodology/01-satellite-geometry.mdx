# Satellite Geometry

MH370's last known electronic signals weren't voice or data transmissions — they were automatic
"handshakes" between the aircraft's satellite terminal and the Inmarsat-3F1 satellite, stationed in
geostationary orbit over the Indian Ocean.

These handshakes are the backbone of the entire search. Each one recorded two key measurements —
**Burst Timing Offset (BTO)** and **Burst Frequency Offset (BFO)** — that let us work backwards
to constrain the aircraft's position and motion.

## The satellite

Inmarsat-3F1 orbits at approximately 64.5°E longitude in a geostationary orbit — roughly 36,000 km
above the equator. But "geostationary" isn't perfectly stationary. The satellite drifts slightly
north-south and east-west over the course of a day, tracing a small figure-eight pattern in the sky.

This drift matters. A few dozen kilometers of satellite motion translates to measurable differences
in signal timing and frequency. Our model uses cubic Hermite spline interpolation on the PAR5
parametric ephemeris data (Rydberg) to track the satellite's exact position at each handshake time,
validated to within 58 meters against GMAT orbital propagation.

<Accordion title="The Math">

The satellite's 3D position at any time $t$ is interpolated from a table of state vectors
$(x, y, z, \dot{x}, \dot{y}, \dot{z})$ sampled at regular intervals. We use cubic Hermite
interpolation, which matches both position and velocity at each knot:

$$
\mathbf{r}(t) = h_{00}(s)\,\mathbf{r}_k + h_{10}(s)\,\Delta t\,\dot{\mathbf{r}}_k + h_{01}(s)\,\mathbf{r}_{k+1} + h_{11}(s)\,\Delta t\,\dot{\mathbf{r}}_{k+1}
$$

where $s = (t - t_k) / \Delta t$ and $h_{00}, h_{10}, h_{01}, h_{11}$ are the Hermite basis
functions. This ensures smooth, physically consistent trajectories — no jumps in velocity at
interval boundaries.

For times outside the ephemeris table range, we fall back to a sinusoidal approximation of the
satellite's oscillation around its nominal geostationary position.

</Accordion>

<Accordion title="The Code">

```rust
// src-tauri/src/mh370/satellite.rs — cubic Hermite interpolation
let s = (t - t0) / dt;
let s2 = s * s;
let s3 = s2 * s;

let h00 = 2.0 * s3 - 3.0 * s2 + 1.0;
let h10 = s3 - 2.0 * s2 + s;
let h01 = -2.0 * s3 + 3.0 * s2;
let h11 = s3 - s2;

// Interpolate each coordinate
let x = h00 * x0 + h10 * dt * vx0 + h01 * x1 + h11 * dt * vx1;
```

The ephemeris data itself is embedded at compile time from `src/data/i3f1_ephemeris.json`, sourced
from the PAR5 parametric model.

</Accordion>

## Burst Timing Offset (BTO)

The BTO measures the round-trip signal delay between the ground station in Perth, the satellite,
and the aircraft. Since we know the positions of Perth and the satellite precisely, the BTO tells
us how far the aircraft was from the satellite — defining a **ring** on the Earth's surface at a
fixed distance from the satellite's sub-point.

Each handshake produces one ring. Seven handshakes produce seven rings. The aircraft must have been
somewhere on each ring at the corresponding time.

<Accordion title="The Math">

The BTO is related to the total signal path delay. After calibrating against the known-position
ground pings at KLIA (where the aircraft's position is known), we extract the aircraft-to-satellite
slant range:

$$
R_{\text{slant}} = \frac{(\text{BTO} - \Delta_{\text{bias}}) \cdot c}{2}
$$

where $c$ is the speed of light and $\Delta_{\text{bias}}$ is the calibration offset determined
from the pre-departure ping at gate position KLIA.

From the slant range, we compute the surface distance (great-circle arc) from the satellite
sub-point, accounting for Earth's curvature and the aircraft's cruise altitude.

</Accordion>
